Understanding the Condition

What is Endometriosis?

Photo by Daizy Isumi on Unsplash

Endometriosis is a chronic, often debilitating condition where tissue similar to the lining of the uterus grows outside of it — on the ovaries, fallopian tubes, bowel, and other areas of the pelvic cavity.

This displaced tissue acts like normal endometrial tissue — it thickens, breaks down, and bleeds with each menstrual cycle. But because it has no way to exit the body, it becomes trapped, leading to inflammation, scarring, adhesions, and severe pain.

Endometriosis is not just "bad periods." It is a systemic inflammatory disease that can affect nearly every aspect of a person's life — from physical health to mental wellbeing, relationships, careers, and fertility.

Did you know? Endometriosis has been found in every organ of the body, including the lungs, brain, and skin. It is not limited to the reproductive system.

Complex Disease

Endometriosis involves the immune system, hormonal system, and genetic factors. It is not caused by anything a person did or didn't do.

Delayed Diagnosis

On average, it takes 7–10 years from symptom onset to receive a diagnosis, during which many are dismissed or misdiagnosed.

Global Impact

Endometriosis costs the global economy over $80 billion annually in healthcare and lost productivity, yet remains critically underfunded.

Recognize the Signs

Common Symptoms

Endometriosis symptoms vary widely. Some experience severe symptoms while others may have none. Here are the most commonly reported signs.

Chronic Pelvic Pain

Persistent pain in the lower abdomen, pelvis, or lower back that may worsen during menstruation but can occur at any time.

Painful Periods

Severe menstrual cramps (dysmenorrhea) that go far beyond typical discomfort and may not respond to over-the-counter pain relief.

Pain During Intimacy

Deep pain during or after sexual intercourse (dyspareunia), which can significantly impact relationships and quality of life.

Heavy or Irregular Bleeding

Excessively heavy periods (menorrhagia) or bleeding between periods, sometimes accompanied by large clots.

Infertility

Up to 30–50% of those with endometriosis may experience difficulty conceiving. It is one of the leading causes of infertility.

Fatigue & Brain Fog

Chronic exhaustion, difficulty concentrating, and "endo brain fog" that persists regardless of rest or sleep quality.

Digestive Issues

Bloating ("endo belly"), nausea, diarrhea, constipation, and painful bowel movements, especially during periods.

Urinary Symptoms

Pain during urination, increased frequency, urgency, or blood in urine — particularly around menstruation.

Important: Experiencing one or more of these symptoms does not necessarily mean you have endometriosis, but it does warrant a conversation with your healthcare provider. Pain that disrupts your daily life is never normal.

The Path to Answers

How is Endometriosis Diagnosed?

Getting diagnosed is often the hardest part. Here's what the journey may look like.

Photo by Carrie Yang on Unsplash
1

Symptom Recognition

Recognizing that your pain is not normal is the critical first step. Many are taught to dismiss severe period pain as "just part of being a woman."

2

Clinical Evaluation

A healthcare provider will review your medical history, perform a pelvic exam, and assess your symptoms. Keeping a detailed symptom diary can be invaluable.

3

Imaging Studies

Ultrasound and MRI can identify endometriomas (ovarian cysts) and deep infiltrating endometriosis, but cannot detect all forms of the disease.

4

Laparoscopy

The gold standard for definitive diagnosis is laparoscopic surgery — a minimally invasive procedure where a surgeon visually confirms and can biopsy endometrial implants.

5

Histological Confirmation

Tissue samples from surgery are examined under a microscope. A pathology report confirming endometrial-like tissue outside the uterus provides a definitive diagnosis.

Advocating for Yourself

If you feel dismissed, seek a second opinion. Consider finding an endometriosis specialist (excision surgeon). You are the expert on your own body — trust your experience.

Managing the Condition

Treatment Options

While there is currently no cure for endometriosis, several approaches can help manage symptoms and improve quality of life.

Photo by Tahamie Farooqui on Unsplash

Pain Management

  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen)
  • Prescription pain medications
  • Nerve blocks and pain clinics
  • Heat therapy and TENS units

Hormonal Therapies

  • Combined oral contraceptives
  • Progestins and progestogens
  • GnRH agonists and antagonists
  • Hormonal IUDs (e.g., Mirena)
Gold Standard

Excision Surgery

  • Laparoscopic excision by a specialist
  • Removes disease at the root
  • Higher long-term success rates
  • Preserves organs when possible

Complementary Approaches

  • Pelvic floor physical therapy
  • Anti-inflammatory diet changes
  • Acupuncture and massage
  • Mental health support and therapy

Note: Hysterectomy is not a cure for endometriosis. Endometrial implants outside the uterus can continue to cause symptoms even after uterus removal. Treatment should be individualized — work with a knowledgeable specialist to find what works best for you.

Separating Fact from Fiction

Common Myths — Debunked

Myth

"It's just bad periods."

Endometriosis is a full-body inflammatory disease. While painful periods are a common symptom, the condition affects far more than menstruation — including the bowel, bladder, diaphragm, and nervous system.

Myth

"Pregnancy cures endometriosis."

Pregnancy may temporarily suppress symptoms due to hormonal changes, but it does not cure the disease. Many experience symptom return after pregnancy, and some have symptoms throughout.

Myth

"A hysterectomy is a cure."

Removing the uterus does not remove endometrial implants elsewhere in the body. Studies show that up to 40% of those who have a hysterectomy for endo continue to have symptoms.

Myth

"It only affects older women."

Endometriosis has been diagnosed in adolescents as young as 10–11 years old. Symptoms often begin with the first menstrual period. Age should never be a reason to dismiss symptoms.

Myth

"It's caused by something you did."

Endometriosis is not caused by lifestyle choices, diet, exercise, or sexual activity. It has genetic, immunological, and environmental components that are still being researched.

Myth

"If tests are normal, you're fine."

Endometriosis often does not show on blood tests, standard ultrasounds, or physical exams. Normal test results do not mean there is nothing wrong — many need surgical confirmation.

Make a Difference

How You Can Help

Spread Awareness

Share this website, talk about endometriosis, and help break the stigma around chronic pain and menstrual health. Use #EndoAwareness on social media.

Support Someone

If someone you know has endo, believe them. Learn about their condition. Ask how you can help. Sometimes just saying "I believe you" changes everything.

Donate to Research

Endometriosis research is critically underfunded. Donate to organizations like the Endometriosis Foundation of America or fund research directly.

Advocate for Policy

Contact your representatives. Support legislation for research funding, insurance coverage, and workplace protections for those with chronic conditions.

Demand Better Care

Push for endometriosis education in medical schools. Support specialist training. Advocate for shorter diagnostic timelines and access to expert care.

Wear Yellow

Yellow is the color of endometriosis awareness. Wear it in March — and all year — to spark conversations and show solidarity.
